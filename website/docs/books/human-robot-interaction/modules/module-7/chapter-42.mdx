## Lesson: Global Governance and Standardization of Social Robotics

### Introduction to Global Governance in Social Robotics

Global governance of social robotics represents one of the most complex and critical challenges in the field, as these technologies transcend national boundaries in their development, deployment, and impact. Unlike traditional robotics applications that operate in isolated industrial environments, social robots operate in intimate human environments and interact with diverse populations across different cultural, legal, and social contexts. This global reach creates the need for coordinated international approaches to governance, standardization, and ethical oversight that can address the shared challenges of social robotics while respecting diverse cultural values and national sovereignty.

The complexity of global governance in social robotics stems from the intersection of multiple domains including technology regulation, human rights, cultural values, and social policy. Social robotics governance must address technical safety and performance standards while also considering ethical implications, cultural sensitivity, and the social impact of these technologies. The challenge is to create governance frameworks that are robust enough to protect human welfare and rights while flexible enough to accommodate cultural differences and diverse application contexts.

Furthermore, the rapid pace of technological development in social robotics often outstrips the development of governance frameworks, creating periods of regulatory uncertainty and potential risk. Global governance must be adaptive and responsive to technological change while maintaining consistent core principles of safety, ethics, and human dignity. This requires new approaches to international cooperation and standardization that can respond more quickly to technological development than traditional international regulatory processes.

The stakes of global governance in social robotics are particularly high because these technologies can affect fundamental aspects of human life including relationships, social structures, and human identity. The governance decisions made today will shape the development and deployment of social robotics for decades to come, affecting how humans relate to these artificial agents and how these technologies impact human welfare and social connection across the globe.

### International Standards Organizations

#### ISO and IEC Framework
International standardization for social robotics:

**ISO/IEC JTC 1**: The joint technical committee that develops international standards for information and communication technologies, including emerging standards for AI and robotics that have social implications. This committee is developing standards for AI systems that may apply to social robotics applications.

**ISO Technical Committee 299**: The specific committee focused on robotics that develops international standards for robot safety, performance, and social interaction. This includes standards for personal care robots and collaborative robots that have social interaction components.

**IEC Standards**: The International Electrotechnical Commission's standards for electrical and electronic systems that may apply to social robotics, particularly safety and performance standards.

**ISO 13482**: International standard for safety requirements for personal care robots that interact with humans in daily life activities, addressing social robotics applications in care contexts.

#### IEEE Global Standards
Professional organization standards for social robotics:

**IEEE Standards Association**: The IEEE's standards development organization that addresses ethical considerations in AI and robotics through standards like IEEE 7000 series on ethical design of autonomous and intelligent systems.

**Ethics in Action Committee**: IEEE's initiative that develops standards and guidelines for ethical implementation of AI and robotics systems including social robotics applications.

**Robot Ethics Standards**: Specific IEEE standards and guidelines for robot ethics that apply to social robotics applications and their governance.

**AI Transparency Standards**: Standards for transparency and explainability in AI systems that apply to social robotics systems with AI components.

#### UN and International Organizations
Global governance bodies addressing social robotics:

**UN International Telecommunication Union (ITU)**: The UN agency that addresses telecommunications and AI standards that may apply to social robotics communication and interaction systems.

**UNESCO**: The UN agency addressing ethical implications of AI and robotics in education, culture, and human development that affect social robotics applications.

**World Health Organization (WHO)**: The international health organization that may develop standards and guidelines for healthcare social robotics applications.

**International Labour Organization (ILO)**: The organization addressing the impact of robotics on employment and labor relations that may affect social robotics applications in workplace contexts.

#### Regional Standardization Bodies
Regional approaches to social robotics standards:

**European Committee for Standardization (CEN)**: European standardization body that develops standards for robotics and AI that may include social robotics considerations.

**Pacific Area Standards Congress (PASCO)**: Forum for ISO member bodies in the Asia-Pacific region to coordinate standards development including robotics standards.

**African Regional Organization for Standardization (ARSO)**: Organization coordinating standards development across African countries including technology standards.

**Pan American Standards Commission (COPANT)**: Organization coordinating standards across the Americas including robotics and AI standards.
